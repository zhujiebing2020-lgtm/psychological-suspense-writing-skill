# Chapter Process

Use this when drafting a new chapter or expanding a thin chapter.

## Before Writing

Read the previous 1-2 chapters, the current outline entry, and relevant references.

Decide:

- chapter question: what must the reader want answered?
- evidence movement: what new thing is learned?
- emotional movement: who loses control, who gains leverage?
- body node: what body stimulus appears or returns?
- relationship scene: which relationship changes?
- viewpoint texture: whose perception cracks the dominant version?
- ending hook: what image, sentence, or discovery should remain?

## Draft Shape

Recommended structure:

1. Open with pressure, not generic action, when literary weight matters.
2. Establish concrete space and bodily condition.
3. Introduce evidence or a person with procedural plausibility.
4. Let the scene reveal a relationship, not only a clue.
5. Insert one memory, recording, object, or physical echo only if it changes the present.
6. End on a clean hook. Do not stack multiple major discoveries.

## Expansion Strategy

When a chapter is too thin, add one of:

- a flashback that reframes present evidence
- a work/procedure scene that exposes the protagonist's instinct
- a care scene that turns tenderness into control
- a witness's moral compromise
- a bodily memory belonging to the absent/dead/missing person
- external pressure from police/procedure/family/institution
- a relationship-origin scene that later curdles

Do not pad with explanation. Add scenes with pressure.

## Thin Chapter Diagnosis

If a chapter is short, ask which load is missing:

- no present scene: build location and immediate action
- no relationship movement: add a scene between two people
- no body pressure: add sensation, gesture, trace, or object
- no suspense movement: add evidence, source, access, or contradiction
- no viewpoint texture: add a short perception crack from another character or material form
- no hook: sharpen the last image or question
